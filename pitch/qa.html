Briefing notes for school leadership
Anticipated questions & honest answers Prepared for deputies, heads of teaching & learning, and faculty heads
The honest framing: ProofReady is not a teacher-replacement, a marking tool, or a writing assistant. It's a formative-feedback tool for drafts, designed to give every student the kind of detailed, criteria-based written feedback an experienced teacher would give if they had time to read every draft a week before it's due. The questions below are the ones we expect, and the answers we stand behind.

Pedagogy & teacher value

Can't students just use ChatGPT on their own?
They already do, quietly, with no syllabus alignment, no marking criteria, no NESA verb depth, and often with feedback that's wrong or generic. ProofReady is a controlled, teacher-set context: the teacher provides the question and the marking criteria, and the feedback is anchored to those.
Isn't a teacher's feedback better than AI feedback?
Yes, for the drafts a teacher has time to read carefully. The reality of HSC marking is that many students don't get individualised written feedback on a draft before they submit. ProofReady doesn't compete with the teacher giving thoughtful feedback in class; it simply provides all students with high-quality feedback, even if they submit their draft the day before it's due at 11pm.
Won't this de-skill teachers?
It removes the lowest-leverage part of marking, pointing out the same paragraph-3-needs-evidence comment for the 17th time, and frees the teacher to do the highest-leverage part: targeted in-class teaching of the gaps that show up across the cohort. ProofReady includes a class-level synthesis view exactly for this: it identifies common weaknesses across the class so the teacher can address them in one lesson rather than 30 individual margins.
Won't students use it to polish their work without actually reading the feedback?
ProofReady does not rewrite the student's work, suggest replacement sentences, or produce text the student can paste in. The feedback explicitly tells the student what needs work and why, not how to fix it. To polish, the student has to read, understand, and act. That's the point of formative feedback.
How is this different from a writing assistant or a plagiarism tool?
A writing assistant generates content; ProofReady refuses to generate content (this is hard-coded in the prompt rules). A plagiarism tool detects copying after the fact; ProofReady acts before submission to help students improve their own work. The closest analogue is what a good marking teacher does in the margin of a draft. But every student gets it, and within a minute.
Won't students cheat by submitting AI-generated work and getting feedback that polishes it?
Academic integrity is the school's policy domain. ProofReady doesn't change it. Three things help: (1) feedback is on the draft, not the final, so it complements the school's existing process; (2) the tool refuses to rewrite content, so it can't "polish" anything; (3) teacher visibility: every submission is visible to the teacher, draft history is preserved, so a student who suddenly produces vastly better work shows it in their draft trail.

Quality & accuracy

AI is generally bad at English feedback. What makes yours different?
Three concrete things. One: the system prompt loads English-Advanced-specific terminology with definitions and common misuses (persona vs. author, theme vs. motif, characterisation as construction not description). Two: the marker-voice layer is calibrated against NESA Notes from the Marking Centre 2021–2024 specifically for English Advanced and English Standard. Three: three independent passes (holistic feedback, criterion-by-criterion, and inline annotations on the student's actual sentences), so a generic answer can't survive all three.
AI hallucinates. What if it cites a NESA outcome that doesn't exist?
The model is given the actual NESA glossary, the student's actual marking criteria (provided by the teacher), and explicit instructions never to invent references. The teacher's criteria are the ground truth; feedback is anchored to those, not to the model's training data. We've also forbidden the model from making band predictions or mark estimates, which is where most low-quality AI feedback creates problems.

Safety & wellbeing

Won't students become dependent on AI feedback?
A real concern, which is why ProofReady caps drafts at three per task. After that, the next conversation is with the teacher. The feedback also includes a self-check question on every submission, designed to push the student to develop their own revision instinct rather than wait for the system to tell them what to fix.

Privacy, data & continuity

Where does student data go? Is Anthropic training on student writing?
No. Anthropic's API policy excludes API inputs and outputs from model training. Drafts are sent over encrypted connections, processed for the time it takes to generate feedback, and then stored only in our Supabase Sydney-region database for review. Full sub-processor list, locations, and breach-notification process at proofready.app/compliance.
What if you go out of business? What happens to our data?
Schools own their data and can request a full export or deletion at any time during or after the pilot. If ProofReady ever wound down, the same applies: we'd notify schools, give 60 days' notice, and provide a full export. Nothing is locked in.

Logistics & rollout

Won't this just be more work for teachers to learn?
Setup is about ten minutes per teacher: create a class, paste the question and marking criteria, click publish. Students join with a code. There is no other configuration, no integration with the school's LMS, and no IT involvement required.
